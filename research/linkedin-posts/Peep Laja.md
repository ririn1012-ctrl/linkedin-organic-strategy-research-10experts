## Post 1

### How to publish an original research study in a week

**Date:** ~Q1 2024

**Format:** Video + text summary

**Engagement:** ~1,200 reactions, 34 comments

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/peeplaja_how-to-publish-an-original-research-study-ugcPost-7170515922376450048-K3Gh

### Post

> How to publish an original research study in a week? (Or every week)
>
> The process:
>
> Define the goal - What do you want to know? What insight would your buyers find valuable?
>
> Run the survey on Wynter - Results back in 12-48 hours from real B2B buyers
>
> AI highlights trends - We pull patterns from the open-ended responses
>
> A writer crafts the content - One clear takeaway per finding, not just raw data
>
> A designer creates the PDF - Shareable, branded, downloadable
>
> Our B2B Buyer Journey study cost ~$1,000 total. We got a completely unique asset that:
>
> Generated backlinks
>
> Was cited in industry newsletters
>
> Fueled a quarter of LinkedIn content
>
> Became a sales enablement piece
>
> Original research is the highest-ROI content investment in B2B.

### Why this post works

One of the most practically useful process posts in B2B content strategy. The total cost ($1,000) makes it accessible. Lists the downstream benefits (backlinks, newsletter citations, social content, sales enablement) which gives a marketer everything they need to justify it to a CFO.

`Pattern: How-to title → numbered process → total cost anchor → downstream benefits list → ROI thesis`

## Post 2

### Almost every B2B SaaS website has images

**Date:** January 2022 (still highly cited)

**Format:** Text post with finding

**Engagement:** ~1,800 reactions, 172 comments

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/peeplaja_almost-every-b2b-saas-website-has-images-activity-6885291883032055808-yGRd

### Post

> Almost every B2B SaaS website has images or illustrations of some kind.
>
> What should they be?
>
> A pattern that comes up again and again in Wynter tests for B2B:
>
> Generic stock photos and generic flat illustrations both perform poorly.
>
> What performs well: screenshots of the actual product in use.
>
> Why: buyers want to understand what they're buying. They want to see what using the product looks like. Abstract visuals don't help them evaluate.
>
> When you show a real screenshot - especially with real data, real workflows, real names (blurred or fake) - trust scores go up.
>
> The implication: your best visual asset is your product. Stop hiding it behind mockups and "lifestyle" photography.

### Why this post works

Backed by actual Wynter test data (not just opinion). Challenges a widespread B2B design convention with a specific, actionable alternative. High save rate from designers and product marketers who can immediately apply this.

`Pattern: Question setup → research finding → reason for the finding → specific visual pattern that works → implication with clear action`

## Post 3

### Most B2B content is indistinguishable from competitor content

**Date:** ~Q3 2024

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~2,400 reactions, 290 comments

### Post

> Most B2B content is indistinguishable from competitor content.
>
> Same topics. Same advice. Same frameworks. Same "5 tips" format.
>
> This is because most content is produced to fill a calendar, not to win a market.
>
> The companies whose content actually drives revenue have one thing in common:
>
> They publish what nobody else can.
>
> This can mean:
>
> Original research only you have access to
>
> Case studies with specific numbers your category avoids
>
> Opinions that would make half your market uncomfortable
>
> Processes from inside your actual operation
>
> Generic content makes you invisible. Differentiated content makes you memorable.
>
> You can't out-produce your competitors. You can out-differentiate them.

### Why this post works

Laja is doing exactly what he describes: taking a non-consensus position and backing it up. "You can't out-produce your competitors. You can out-differentiate them." is a highly quotable closer that works as a standalone principle.

`Pattern: Problem diagnosis → root cause → what companies with results have in common → four types of differentiable content → principle contrast → memorable closer`

## Post 4

### The B2B Buyer Journey Study findings

**Date:** ~Q1 2024

**Format:** Data post (linked to research report)

**Engagement:** ~1,500 reactions, 82 comments

**Source Context:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/peeplaja_b2b-buyer-journey-study-by-wynter-activity-7171847547144122368-8t1V/

### Post

> We surveyed 500 B2B buyers on how they actually research and decide.
>
> Key findings:

#### On awareness

> 67% first heard about a vendor through peer recommendations or industry communities
>
> Only 18% through a search engine as their first touchpoint

#### On content

> Case studies with specific numbers are read. "Industry-leading results" are ignored.
>
> The majority research vendors on LinkedIn before ever visiting the website
>
> Reviews on G2/Capterra matter more than you think - even for enterprise

#### On the decision

> More than half said a champion inside the company drove the final push
>
> The demo is still critical - but it happens later than vendors think
>
> The most common barrier to buying: unclear differentiation between options

#### Implication

> The implication: your marketing should create awareness in communities, build your LinkedIn presence, and make differentiation obvious.
>
> Full report linked below.

### Why this post works

The data Laja surfaces is from his own proprietary Wynter research - nobody else has it. Each bullet is a specific finding with a percentage, which respects the reader's intelligence. The implication at the end converts the data to action without being prescriptive.

`Pattern: Survey authority → finding clusters by journey stage → each finding is specific (not generic) → single synthesis implication → CTA to full report`

## Post 5

### What makes a B2B company actually win

**Date:** ~Q4 2024

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~3,100 reactions, 410 comments

### Post

> What makes a B2B company actually win in a crowded market?
>
> Not better features. Everybody says they have better features.
>
> Not better price. Competing on price is a race to the bottom.
>
> Not better service. Table stakes.
>
> The companies that win long-term win on the strength of their category position.
>
> Category position = who you are, for whom, against what alternatives, in a way that is distinct and memorable.
>
> The test: Can your ideal customer explain the difference between you and your top 3 competitors in one sentence?
>
> If they can't, your positioning hasn't worked yet.
>
> Your product can be excellent and your positioning can still be broken.

### Why this post works

Uses elimination structure (not X, not Y, not Z) before giving the real answer - very aligned with April Dunford's positioning language, which is intentional (they cross-reference each other frequently). The test at the end ("can your ideal customer explain it in one sentence") is immediately usable.

`Pattern: Question → three "not this" eliminations → real answer → definition of answer → simple diagnostic test → closing caveat`
