## Post 1 - "SaaS metrics are broken"

**Date:** ~Q4 2024

**Format:** Carousel (PDF) - 8 slides

**Engagement:** ~3,800 reactions, 490 comments

**Source reference:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kyle-poyar_new-saas-metrics-playbook-activity-7130931270166949889-z78j

### Hook slide

> SaaS metrics are broken. Here's the new playbook.

### Slide 2

> ARR is still the north star. But it's not enough anymore.
>
> The question isn't just "how much ARR?" - it's "how healthy is that ARR?"

### Slide 3 - The old playbook

> Land → Expand → Hope
>
> MQL → SQL → Close
>
> NRR as a lagging indicator

### Slide 4 - The problem

> CAC payback stretching to 30+ months in many SaaS businesses
>
> "Magic number" efficiency scores dropping across the board
>
> Growth at all costs → growth at reasonable costs

### Slide 5 - New metrics that matter

> ARR per employee (signal of efficiency)
>
> Payback period by segment (not blended)
>
> Logo retention + net revenue retention (you need both)
>
> Pipeline coverage by motion (PLG vs. SLG)

### Slide 6 - PLG benchmark data (from OpenView 2024 survey)

> Median time to convert freemium → paid: 18 days
>
> Top-quartile PLG companies: 110%+ NRR
>
> Median PLG CAC payback: 18 months vs 28 months for sales-led

### Slide 7 - What to measure instead

Replace vanity metrics with signal metrics:

> MQLs → Activated users
>
> Leads → Pipeline by source
>
> Traffic → Engaged ICP visitors

### Slide 8 - Takeaway

> The companies winning in 2025 optimize for efficient growth, not growth at any cost. Track what actually predicts revenue health.

### Why this post works

Poyar's carousels consistently outperform his text posts by 3–5x. This one works because it:

* Challenges a consensus assumption with data
* Gives practitioners specific numbers to benchmark against
* Packages a complex idea into shareable slides

**Pattern:**
`Provocative hook → old way vs. new way → proprietary benchmark data → actionable replacement framework`

## Post 2 - "Per-seat pricing is dying"

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Text post + data callout

**Engagement:** ~4,200 reactions, 570 comments

### Post

> Per-seat pricing is dying.
>
> Not because it's wrong. Because AI broke it.
>
> When one person with an AI copilot does the work of 5, "per seat" means you get paid less as your customers get more value.
>
> The companies that figure out outcome-based pricing in 2025 will win. The ones that don't will watch their NRR compress.
>
> Three pricing models gaining ground:
>
> Usage-based: you pay for what you use (tokens, API calls, tasks completed)
>
> Outcome-based: you pay based on results (leads generated, deals closed, errors fixed)
>
> Hybrid: usage floor + outcome upside
>
> Which one you choose depends on what value you can actually prove and measure.
>
> The hard part isn't choosing. It's building the instrumentation to track outcomes.

### Why this post works

Identifies an industry-level shift (AI disrupting per-seat SaaS) and connects it to a practical business decision. High engagement from both SaaS founders and buyers who recognize the tension.

**Pattern:**
`Strong claim → causation explanation → stakes → 3-option taxonomy → implementation truth`

## Post 3 - "What separates PLG companies that scale from those that plateau"

**Date:** ~Q2 2024

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~2,900 reactions, 380 comments

### Post

> What separates PLG companies that scale from those that plateau?
>
> After looking at data from 500+ SaaS companies:
>
> It's not the free tier.
>
> It's not the onboarding flow.
>
> It's not even the product.
>
> It's whether the product creates a natural reason to share.
>
> Virality isn't a feature you add. It's a property of the core use case.
>
> Figma: the design lives in the browser. Sharing is the action.
>
> Loom: the video is the message. Sending is the action.
>
> Notion: the doc is collaborative. Inviting is the action.
>
> If sharing the output of your product requires the recipient to have your product - you have virality baked in.
>
> If it doesn't, you can still build PLG. But you'll need a stronger sales assist.

### Why this post works

Takes a topic everyone talks about (PLG) and adds a specific analytical framework that most practitioners haven't articulated this cleanly. The Figma/Loom/Notion examples are concrete and immediately recognizable.

**Pattern:**
`Question hook → data authority claim → surprise "it's not X" → real answer → 3 named examples with pattern → implication`

## Post 4 - "Most startups launch the wrong product for the wrong buyer"

**Date:** ~Q3 2024

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~2,100 reactions, 290 comments

### Post

> Most startups launch the wrong product for the wrong buyer.
>
> Not because they're stupid. Because they talk to people who tell them what they want to hear.
>
> The people who say "yes, I'd pay for that" in a user interview are often not the people who actually pull out a credit card.
>
> The research showing the biggest gap between "stated" and "revealed" preference:
>
> Features related to collaboration ("I'd love team features!")
>
> Compliance features ("We'd definitely need SOC 2")
>
> Integrations ("We'd need this to connect to X")
>
> What people reveal through actual behavior:
>
> They want the simplest version that solves the core problem
>
> They pay for things that make them look good or save them from embarrassment
>
> They upgrade when the cost of NOT upgrading is obvious
>
> Talk to customers. But watch what they do more than what they say.

### Why this post works

Uses Wynter/research context (buyer testing) to challenge a widespread B2B product assumption. The "stated vs. revealed preference" framework is academically grounded and practically useful.

**Pattern:**
`Broad diagnosis → remove blame → research finding → gap between stated/revealed → behavioral truth → memorable closer`
