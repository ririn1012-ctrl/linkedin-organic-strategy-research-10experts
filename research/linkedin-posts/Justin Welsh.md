## Post 1

### How to get started as a solopreneur (even if you have less than 5k followers)

**Date:** Q4 2023 (evergreen, regularly resurfaces)

**Format:** Text list post

**Engagement:** ~8,200 reactions, 821 comments

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_how-to-get-started-as-a-solopreneur-even-activity-7137428886270709760-vGV6

### Post

> How to get started as a solopreneur:
>
> (even if you have less than 5k followers)
>
> Pick a niche you know well
>
> Create content that serves that niche
>
> Give away your best ideas for free
>
> Build a free newsletter to collect emails
>
> Engage with everyone who engages with you
>
> Study the posts that perform well
>
> Do more of what works
>
> Do less of what doesn't
>
> Build a small product to test your audience
>
> Iterate based on feedback
>
> Never stop posting
>
> The people who quit don't fail. They just stop.

### Why this post works

Deceptively simple - each item is one line and requires no explanation. The pacing (11 steps, one per line) creates rhythm. The closing line ("The people who quit don't fail. They just stop.") is a quotable that gets screenshotted and reshared independently.

`Pattern: Listicle structure → numbered steps with no elaboration → memorable one-line closer`

## Post 2

### 2025 LinkedIn Profile Optimization Guide

**Date:** Q4 2024

**Format:** Carousel/document post

**Engagement:** ~11,400 reactions, 1,900 comments

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_2025-linkedin-profile-optimization-guide-activity-7275571084144328705-4TR0

### Post

#### Hook

> Your LinkedIn profile isn't a resume. It's a sales page.

#### Section 1: The Banner

> Your banner image is the first thing people see. Most people waste it with generic images or leave it blank.
>
> Use it to state: Who you help → What you help them do → How to work with you

#### Section 2: The Headline

> Stop: "Founder | Speaker | Author | Coach"
>
> Start: "I help [specific person] achieve [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe]"
>
> Your headline is indexed by LinkedIn search. Generic headlines get zero inbound.

#### Section 3: The About Section

> First 3 lines are above the fold. Make them count.
>
> Line 1: Who you help
>
> Line 2: What you do for them
>
> Line 3: What they get (result/outcome)
>
> Then: social proof, story, how to contact you.

#### Section 4: Featured Section

> This is the most underused section on LinkedIn.
>
> Put your best piece of content here. Your most-saved post. Your newsletter link. Your top YouTube video.

#### Section 5: The Activity Section

> People check your recent activity before they decide to follow you.
>
> If your last post was 3 months ago, you lost them.
>
> Post at least 2–3x per week or don't expect inbound.

#### Closing

> Your profile is open 24/7. Optimize it once, benefit forever.

### Why this post works

Enormous shareability because almost everyone's LinkedIn profile is underoptimized. The "resume vs. sales page" reframe in the hook is the most-quoted line. Each section gives one actionable change. High save rate = algorithmic boost.

`Pattern: Reframe hook → section-by-section breakdown → "stop/start" contrast for each element → compounding benefit closer`

## Post 3

### I create a week of content in 4 hours

**Date:** ~Q2 2024

**Format:** System breakdown

**Engagement:** ~7,800 reactions, 960 comments

### Post

> I create a week of content in 4 hours.
>
> Here's my exact system:
>
> Hour 1: Capture (Sunday morning)
>
> Open Notion. Dump every idea from the past week.
>
> Shower thoughts. Conversations. Things I noticed. Things that annoyed me.
>
> Don't filter. Just capture.
>
> Hour 2: Select + Outline (Sunday afternoon)
>
> Pick 5 ideas worth posting.
>
> For each, write: Hook → Core idea → Proof → CTA
>
> Takes 5 minutes per post.
>
> Hour 3: Write (Monday morning)
>
> Turn the outlines into actual posts.
>
> Each post: under 200 words.
>
> Rule: if I can cut it, I cut it.
>
> Hour 4: Schedule + Repurpose (Monday afternoon)
>
> Schedule all 5 posts in Hypefury.
>
> Take the 2 best and turn them into newsletter sections.
>
> Take the 1 best and turn it into a thread for X.
>
> Total: 4 hours. 5 LinkedIn posts. 1 newsletter. 1 thread.
>
> This is the system. The system is the business.

### Why this post works

Fulfills a core promise (reducing content creation to a manageable system) with extreme specificity. Each "hour" label creates structure. Names the actual tools (Notion, Hypefury). "The system is the business" is his most-repeated phrase.

`Pattern: Bold claim → breakdown by time block → specific tools named → output per block → memorable thesis statement`

## Post 4

### The LinkedIn algorithm rewards one thing above everything else

**Date:** ~Q4 2024

**Format:** Short text post

**Engagement:** ~4,300 reactions, 580 comments

### Post

> The LinkedIn algorithm rewards one thing above everything else:
>
> Dwell time.
>
> Not likes. Not comments. Not shares (though those help).
>
> If someone stops scrolling and reads your entire post, the algorithm interprets that as quality content and shows it to more people.
>
> How to engineer dwell time:
>
> Use line breaks after every 1-2 sentences (forces people to scroll)
>
> Start with a hook that creates tension or curiosity
>
> Write content that requires effort to read - not because it's hard, but because it's worth it
>
> Don't give away the ending in the first line
>
> The goal isn't to go viral. The goal is to make people stop.

### Why this post works

Demystifies the algorithm by naming one mechanism (dwell time) instead of providing a checklist. The irony that this post itself uses the techniques it describes is noted by commenters (which drives more comments).

`Pattern: Algorithm claim → single mechanism → explain why → 4 tactical applications → reframe success metric`

## Post 5

### The PAIPS formula for LinkedIn posts

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Framework post

**Engagement:** ~6,100 reactions, 840 comments

### Post

> I've written 3,000+ LinkedIn posts.
>
> After testing everything, here's the structure that works most consistently:
>
> P - Pain
>
> Start with the problem your reader has right now.
>
> "Most people who post on LinkedIn never get any traction."
>
> A - Agitate
>
> Make the pain feel real.
>
> "They spend hours writing. Get 12 likes. Give up."
>
> I - Insight
>
> Give them the reframe.
>
> "The problem isn't effort. It's structure."
>
> P - Proof
>
> Show it works.
>
> "I've used this framework for 2 years. Gone from 0 to 600k followers."
>
> S - Solution
>
> Tell them what to do.
>
> "Use PAIPS on every post. Start with Pain. End with Solution."
>
> Copy this framework. Customize for your niche.

### Why this post works

Named frameworks are more shareable than unnamed ones. PAIPS is memorable, applies to B2B content immediately, and the post demonstrates itself (it uses PAIPS while teaching PAIPS). Very high save rate.

`Pattern: Authority claim → framework acronym → each letter demonstrated with one-line example → permission to copy`
