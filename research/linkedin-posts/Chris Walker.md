## Post 1 - "Your MQL model is killing your pipeline"

**Date:** ~Q3 2024

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~3,200 reactions, 510 comments

### Post

> Your MQL model is killing your pipeline.
>
> Here's how:
>
> You optimize for volume of form fills
>
> You measure success by MQL quantity
>
> Sales gets handed low-intent contacts
>
> Sales ignores them (correctly)
>
> Marketing gets blamed for "bad leads"
>
> Marketing runs more top-of-funnel to generate more MQLs
>
> Repeat
>
> The issue isn't your ads. It's the model.
>
> People don't decide to buy B2B software because they downloaded an ebook. They decide after they've consumed enough content, talked to peers, and built conviction over weeks or months.
>
> Your MQL captures the moment they're willing to exchange an email. Not the moment they're ready to buy.
>
> Replace MQL with pipeline coverage and self-reported attribution. Get much closer to reality.

### Why this post works

Uses a numbered cycle to show a systemic failure - the loop format makes the dysfunction visceral. Lands on a specific tactical replacement (pipeline coverage + self-reported attribution) rather than just stating the problem.

**Pattern:**
`Bold claim → numbered dysfunction cycle → root cause explanation → buyer reality → specific replacement`

## Post 2 - "What dark social actually means"

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~2,800 reactions, 360 comments

### Post

> Dark social isn't a mysterious black box.
>
> It's just conversations your analytics can't see.
>
> Slack groups. Discord servers. Private LinkedIn DMs. Text messages between colleagues. Peer recommendations over lunch.
>
> "How did you hear about us?"
>
> → "I honestly don't remember, I've seen you everywhere."
>
> That "everywhere" is dark social working.
>
> The implication for B2B content:
>
> Stop creating content that drives clicks. Start creating content that drives conversations.
>
> The goal is not traffic. The goal is to become the thing people reference when they're already deciding to buy.

### Why this post works

Demystifies a buzzword by giving it concrete examples. The direct-message dialogue format makes abstract attribution tangible. The implication ("clicks vs. conversations") gives marketers a decision framework.

**Pattern:**
`Demystify the buzzword → specific examples → customer voice moment → implication → reframe success metric`

## Post 3 - "The B2B buying journey looks nothing like your funnel"

**Date:** ~Q2 2024

**Format:** Text post with description of visual

**Engagement:** ~4,600 reactions, 640 comments

### Post

> The B2B buying journey looks nothing like your funnel.
>
> Your funnel:
>
> Awareness → Consideration → Decision → Purchase
>
> The actual journey:
>
> See your content while scrolling LinkedIn → Forget about it → See your brand mentioned in a Slack group → Look you up → Read a few posts → See a podcast episode with your CEO → Tell a colleague → They look you up → Someone in procurement googles you → Demo → More internal discussions → Maybe buy after 6 months
>
> No entry point. No linear steps. Multiple people. Multiple touchpoints. Over a long time.
>
> Your attribution model gives credit to Google because that's where someone typed your name into a search bar.
>
> The content that actually created the buying intent was the podcast. The Slack mention. The LinkedIn post. The colleague recommendation.
>
> You're optimizing for the wrong signal.

### Why this post works

The contrast format (your funnel vs. the actual journey) is Walker's signature move. The run-on sentence describing real buyer behavior is intentionally chaotic - it mirrors how buying actually feels. Extremely shareable because it validates what demand gen teams know is true but can't say to their CFO.

**Pattern:**
`Contrast setup → short clean version of wrong model → messy accurate version → attribution gap → wrong signal identified`

## Post 4 - "Stop creating gated content"

**Date:** ~Q4 2024

**Format:** Short text post

**Engagement:** ~5,100 reactions, 790 comments

### Post

> Stop creating gated content.
>
> You're not capturing demand. You're identifying people early enough in their journey that they'll ignore every follow-up email you send.
>
> Your competitors who publish their best thinking ungated are building trust while you're building a list of people who want to read a PDF.
>
> In 2025: the cost of your knowledge is not the lead. The cost is your best ideas sitting behind a form.
>
> Give it away. The buyers who are ready will find you.

### Why this post works

Short, punchy, and directly challenges a standard B2B marketing practice. Gets high engagement because half the people sharing it are forwarding it to their CMO. The "cost" reframe at the end is the most memorable line.

**Pattern:**
`Directive opener → show why it backfires → show competitor advantage → reframe the cost → close with counter-intuitive truth`

## Post 5 - "I've run 2,000+ B2B marketing experiments. Here's what works."

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Carousel (PDF) - 6 slides

**Engagement:** ~6,400 reactions, 840 comments

### Hook slide

> After 2,000+ experiments with B2B companies: here's what actually drives pipeline.

### Slide 2 - What doesn't work (but most companies still do)

> Generic ebooks behind forms
>
> Cold email to purchased lists
>
> Paid search as primary demand gen
>
> Weekly blog posts with no distribution plan

### Slide 3 - What works

> Thought leadership from internal experts (founder, CEO, dept. heads)
>
> Podcasts - especially guest appearances on podcasts your ICP already listens to
>
> Community participation (not community creation - too slow)
>
> LinkedIn organic from 3-5 people at the company, not just the brand page

### Slide 4 - The pipeline signal that matters

> Self-reported attribution + pipeline coverage by segment
>
> Ask every inbound lead:
>
> "Where did you first hear about us?"
>
> The answer is almost never the channel you think.

### Slide 5 - Timing truth

> Most B2B content creates demand 6–18 months before the purchase.
>
> You're planting trees, not harvesting them.
>
> Companies that stop content when pipeline dips are cutting down the orchard.

### Slide 6 - The one change

> If you do nothing else: get one executive posting on LinkedIn twice a week.
>
> No company posts. Real human perspective.
>
> This single change has more pipeline impact than most paid campaigns.

### Why this post works

"2,000+ experiments" is a massive credibility anchor. The carousel format lets each slide land individually. The "planting trees" metaphor in slide 5 is the most-quoted single idea from this post. The final slide delivers one executable action, not a list.

**Pattern:**
`Authority anchor → what doesn't work → what works → how to measure → timing reality → one executable action`
