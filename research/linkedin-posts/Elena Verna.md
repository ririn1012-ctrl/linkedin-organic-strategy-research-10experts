## Post 1

### B2B and B2C growth are fundamentally different

**Date:** ~Q3 2024

**Format:** Carousel/slide framework

**Engagement:** ~4,900 reactions, 640 comments

### Post

#### Hook

> B2B and B2C growth are fundamentally different. Here's the framework nobody shows you.

#### Slide 2: The core difference

> B2C: one user, one decision, one payment method
>
> B2B: multiple users, multiple stakeholders, procurement processes, contracts
>
> This isn't just a sales complexity difference. It's a fundamentally different growth loop.

#### Slide 3: B2C growth loop

> Product value → user retention → organic virality → new users → more product value

#### Slide 4: B2B growth loop

> Product value (to user) → user retention → user advocacy → internal expansion → new team → procurement → contract → more product value
>
> The B2B loop has a procurement step that can kill momentum even when users love your product.

#### Slide 5: What this means for PLG

> Real B2B PLG isn't "just let users sign up."
>
> It's: help users prove value fast enough that they can fight for you internally.
>
> Your job is to turn users into internal champions before the procurement wall hits.

#### Slide 6: The segmentation mistake

> Most SaaS companies have both B2B and B2C users in the same product.
>
> They run one growth strategy for both.
>
> This is the mistake. The growth motions are different. Segment them.

#### Slide 7: Framework

> B2C users → optimize for immediate, individual value → measure DAU/WAU
>
> B2B users → optimize for team value + expansion → measure activated teams, not activated users

### Why this post works

Verna's clearest pedagogical strength: making the invisible obvious. The B2B/B2C distinction seems obvious until she shows you the procurement wall in the loop. High share rate among growth practitioners who manage both user types.

`Pattern: Framework hook → core difference → two loops visualized → implication → common mistake → specific segmentation fix`

## Post 2

### Your PLG motion is probably fake

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~5,700 reactions, 890 comments

### Post

> Your PLG motion is probably fake.
>
> Here's the checklist:

#### Real PLG

> ✅ Your product solves a real problem users feel immediately
>
> ✅ Users understand the value within their first session
>
> ✅ Usage naturally expands to teammates (virality is a product property)
>
> ✅ Upgrades are triggered by hitting value, not by hitting limits
>
> ✅ Your best customers found you, not the other way around

#### Fake PLG

> ❌ You have a free tier but sales still calls every signup within 24 hours
>
> ❌ The "aha moment" requires a 45-minute onboarding call
>
> ❌ You measure "activated users" by email confirmation, not product engagement
>
> ❌ Your free plan is designed to frustrate, not to prove value
>
> ❌ You call yourself PLG because Slack and Figma do it and you also have users
>
> Run the checklist. Be honest.

### Why this post works

The "real vs. fake" checklist format is one of the most shareable structures on LinkedIn. Each item has teeth - the "fake PLG" items are painfully accurate for most companies claiming PLG. High comment engagement because people either share it as a burn or argue with individual items.

`Pattern: Provocative claim → two-column checklist → real examples under each column → honest closer`

## Post 4

### GTM motion design: the most underrated startup decision

**Date:** ~Q4 2024

**Format:** Framework carousel

**Engagement:** ~3,600 reactions, 480 comments

### Post

#### Hook

> Most startups choose their GTM motion by accident. Here's how to choose it on purpose.

#### Slide 2: The 3 GTM motions

> Product-led: product drives acquisition, conversion, and expansion
>
> Sales-led: humans drive acquisition, conversion, and expansion
>
> Community-led: community drives acquisition, trust, and advocacy

#### Slide 3: How to choose

> The right motion depends on:
>
> ACV (annual contract value)
>
> Buyer sophistication
>
> Product complexity
>
> Time to value
>
> Virality of output
>
> High ACV + complex buyer + long time to value = Sales-led
>
> Low ACV + simple value + immediate output = Product-led
>
> Strong community identity = Community-led

#### Slide 4: Common mistake

> Companies choose PLG because it's fashionable, not because their product and buyer fit it.
>
> PLG requires: product that delivers value without human explanation + buyers who self-serve + a natural expansion motion

#### Slide 5: The hybrid reality

> Most B2B SaaS companies above $10M ARR run a hybrid.
>
> PLG for acquisition → Sales assist for conversion → Customer success for expansion
>
> The mistake is thinking these are separate. They're one coordinated motion.

#### Slide 6: The question

> "What is the most efficient path to value for our buyer?"
>
> Answer that and you've answered the GTM motion question.

### Why this post works

Decision frameworks are highly saved because they're useful in the future (team meetings, strategy sessions). The three-motion taxonomy is clear. The "common mistake" slide adds edge that prevents this from being a generic framework.

`Pattern: Setup (decision made by accident) → taxonomy → decision criteria table → common mistake → hybrid reality → single diagnostic question`

## Post 5

### Everyone is running the same broken content strategy

**Date:** ~Q2 2024

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~3,900 reactions, 520 comments

### Post

> Everyone is running the same broken content strategy:
>
> Blog posts optimized for keywords nobody cares about
>
> Ebooks that collect emails from people who aren't your ICP
>
> LinkedIn posts from the brand account that nobody follows
>
> Webinars with 12 live attendees
>
> "Thought leadership" that teaches nothing
>
> The problem isn't content. It's that this content doesn't reach buyers when they're in a buying motion.
>
> What actually works in 2025:
>
> Your CEO's LinkedIn, not the company page
>
> Podcasts your buyers already listen to (as guests, not your own show)
>
> Short-form video explaining how your product solves a specific problem
>
> Community participation where your buyers ask questions
>
> Customer stories told by customers, in their words
>
> Create content where your buyer already is. Not where your marketing team is comfortable.

### Why this post works

Verna's "list of what's broken" format consistently performs well because every reader recognizes their own company in at least one item. The pivot to "what works" prevents it from being pure cynicism. The closing line ("where your buyer is, not where your marketing team is comfortable") is the quotable closer.

`Pattern: "Everyone does X" → numbered broken list → root cause → what works list → memorable principle closer`
