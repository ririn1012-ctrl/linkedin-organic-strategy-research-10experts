## Post 1

### The first 10 hires across 20 top B2B startups

**Date:** September 2023 (still widely shared)

**Format:** Data post

**Engagement:** ~4,100 reactions, 13,000+ engagements on linked newsletter

**Source:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/lennyrachitsky_the-first-10-hires-across-20-top-b2b-startups-activity-7115004009043660802-jkr9

### Post

> I analyzed the first 10 hires at 20 top B2B startups.
>
> Patterns that surprised me:
>
> What they hired first:
>
> Engineering: almost everyone. Usually hire 1-3 engineers before anything else.
>
> Sales: much earlier than most founders think. Before product-market fit at several.
>
> Customer Success: shockingly early at companies with high NRR.
>
> What they hired late:
>
> Marketing: most B2B startups with strong growth didn't hire their first marketer until $2M+ ARR
>
> HR: almost never before $10M ARR
>
> Finance: also surprisingly late
>
> What this tells us:
>
> The founders who built the most efficient B2B growth engines delayed marketing hires and focused on product + sales first.
>
> Marketing came when they had something worth marketing.
>
> The implication: if you're pre-$2M ARR and your biggest problem is awareness, you probably have a product or sales problem.
>
> Full data in the newsletter (link in comments).

### Why this post works

The research is original - Rachitsky collected it himself from his community. "Surprised me" is an authentic hook that makes the data feel discovered rather than curated. The implication at the end ("if you're pre-$2M and your biggest problem is awareness") reframes the data into a diagnostic.

`Pattern: Research anchor → what surprised me → two clusters (early hires, late hires) → synthesis → diagnostic implication → CTA to full data`

## Post 2

### What separates top product teams from the rest

**Date:** ~Q2 2024

**Format:** Research summary

**Engagement:** ~5,200 reactions, 710 comments

### Post

> I surveyed 200 PMs at top tech companies.
>
> The biggest gap between top-quartile and bottom-quartile product teams:
>
> Top-quartile teams:
>
> ✅ Talk to customers 3+ times per week
>
> ✅ Have a clear "north star" metric the whole team knows
>
> ✅ Kill features regularly - they have a deprecation process
>
> ✅ Say no to stakeholders routinely and have a framework for it
>
> Bottom-quartile teams:
>
> ❌ Talk to customers quarterly at best
>
> ❌ Optimize for different metrics across sub-teams
>
> ❌ Keep every feature shipped ("we might need it later")
>
> ❌ Add scope in response to stakeholder pressure
>
> The variable that most predicted north-star performance: customer conversation frequency.
>
> Not headcount. Not process. Not OKRs. Customer conversations.

### Why this post works

The checkmark/X format makes it visually scannable and immediately shareable by PMs who want to send it to their leadership. The single variable at the end ("customer conversation frequency") gives readers one takeaway to carry.

`Pattern: Survey anchor → quartile split → checklist per group → single differentiating variable → rejects common assumptions`

## Post 3

### The new AI growth playbook for 2026

**Date:** ~Q4 2025

**Format:** Synthesis post

**Engagement:** ~6,800 reactions, 920 comments

**Source Context:** Referenced as one of most-shared SaaS pieces from late 2025

### Post

> Elena Verna joined me to discuss how Lovable hit $200M ARR in under a year with 100 employees.
>
> Here's what changed about the growth playbook:
>
> Old playbook:
>
> Build feature → market it → users adopt
>
> Growth = acquisition + retention
>
> Distribution through SEO, ads, sales
>
> New playbook (AI-native SaaS):
>
> Users co-create with AI → they own the output → they share it
>
> Growth = viral output loops
>
> Distribution through what users build and share
>
> The Lovable insight: when your product creates something users are proud of, they market for you.
>
> The constraint this breaks: you can no longer separate "product" from "distribution." The output is the ad.
>
> Three companies that get this right: Lovable, Perplexity, Cursor.
>
> Three questions to ask about your product:
>
> Is your output shareable?
>
> Do users identify with what they build?
>
> Does sharing the output require others to use the product?
>
> If yes to all three: you have a viral loop. If not: you're still in the old playbook.

### Why this post works

Rachitsky's format of "old playbook vs. new playbook" is one of his most effective structures. Grounding it in a specific company (Lovable, $200M ARR, 100 employees) gives it specificity. The 3-question diagnostic at the end turns a high-level insight into an operational tool.

`Pattern: Anchor to a specific case → old vs. new playbook structure → the core insight in one sentence → the strategic implication → named examples → 3-question self-assessment`

## Post 4

### How to identify your ICP

**Date:** ~Q1 2024

**Format:** Framework post

**Engagement:** ~4,300 reactions, 580 comments

### Post

> Most B2B companies have an ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) document that nobody uses.
>
> Here's how to find your real ICP:
>
> Step 1: Pull your top 20% of customers by NRR
>
> Step 2: Find what they have in common (industry, company size, tech stack, pain profile, buying trigger)
>
> Step 3: Interview 5 of them. Ask: "What were you using before us? What made you decide to change?"
>
> Step 4: Find the repeating pattern in the buying trigger. This is your ICP signal.
>
> The ICP is not who you want to sell to.
>
> It's who already bought and stayed.
>
> Work backward from your happiest, most profitable customers. They'll tell you who your ICP is.
>
> The document isn't the ICP. The pattern is.

### Why this post works

Reframes a common B2B concept (ICP) by distinguishing between aspirational ICP (who we want) and actual ICP (who stayed). The 4-step process is concrete enough to assign to someone tomorrow.

`Pattern: Common practice failure → 4-step backward process → key reframe (not who you want, who stayed) → memorable closer`
