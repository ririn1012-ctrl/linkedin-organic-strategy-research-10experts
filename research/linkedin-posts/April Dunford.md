## Post 1 - "Positioning is not your purpose statement"

**Date:** ~Q2 2024  
**Format:** Text post  
**Engagement:** ~3,400 reactions, 460 comments  

---

Positioning is not your purpose statement.

It's not your vision. It's not your values. It's not "what you stand for."

Positioning is the answer to one specific question: Why should I buy this instead of the alternatives?

That's it. That's the whole job.

When I work with B2B tech companies, I find most of them have positioning that answers every question except that one.

They know what their mission is. They know what they believe. They know their culture.

But when I ask "what are the alternatives and why are you better?" - they struggle.

Start there. Everything else can come later.

---

**Why this post works:**  
Dunford does what great positioning does: defines a concept by what it's not before saying what it is. The direct question "Why should I buy this instead of the alternatives?" is portable and quotable.

**Pattern:** Define by exclusion (not X, not Y, not Z) → razor-sharp positive definition → reveal the real gap → redirect to starting point

## Post 2 - "The five components of positioning"

**Date:** ~Q4 2024  
**Format:** Text post with framework  
**Engagement:** ~4,100 reactions, 510 comments  

---

Most B2B companies think they have a positioning problem. They usually have five.

The five components you have to nail, in order:

1. **Competitive alternatives** - What would your customer do if you didn't exist?
2. **Differentiated capabilities** - What can you do that the alternatives can't?
3. **Value** - What does that difference mean for the customer?
4. **Best-fit customers** - Who cares most about that value?
5. **Market category** - What frame helps the customer understand what you do?

Most teams jump to #5 (category) before they know #1 (alternatives).

That's why so many companies land in a category that makes sense internally but confuses buyers.

Build from the bottom up.

---

**Why this post works:**  
Directly from her *Obviously Awesome* book but distilled to LinkedIn format. The numbered list with bold labels is scannable but each item contains a full conceptual load. The sequence matters - and she says so.

**Pattern:** Problem reframe (one problem is actually five) → ordered framework → common mistake at a specific step → implication

## Post 3 - "Your competitive differentiation might be invisible to your customer"

**Date:** ~Q1 2025  
**Format:** Text post  
**Engagement:** ~2,700 reactions, 310 comments  

---

Your competitive differentiation might be invisible to your customer.

Not because they don't care. Because they can't see it.

I call this the "features that don't look like features" problem.

Examples:
- A data enrichment company with 98% accuracy vs. 85% for competitors - but customers can't verify this before they buy
- A security tool with patented architecture that prevents a whole class of vulnerabilities - but the customer doesn't know what that class is
- A workflow tool that's 4x faster - but the customer has never used anything faster, so 4x means nothing

The solution isn't better marketing copy.

The solution is proof. Evidence. Demos. Free trials. Case studies that show the difference, not just claim it.

Tell them what to look for and show them where to look.

---

**Why this post works:**  
Solves a specific problem that every B2B product marketer has faced. The three examples are concrete and cover different industries, making the pattern obvious. Closes with a prescriptive action, not just an observation.

**Pattern:** Diagnosis → name the syndrome → 3 concrete examples with structure → wrong solution → right solution → specific tactics

## Post 4 - "The best sales pitch I've ever seen"

**Date:** ~Q2 2024  
**Format:** Storytelling post  
**Engagement:** ~5,600 reactions, 730 comments  

---

The best sales pitch I've ever seen started with this line:

"Before I tell you anything about us, I want to understand your world."

And then the rep asked two questions:
1. "What are you using today to solve this problem, and what do you like about it?"
2. "What's the one thing about your current solution that's costing you the most?"

Twenty minutes of listening. Notes.

Then the rep said: "Based on what you've told me, here's where we're different from what you're using today - and here's why that matters for the specific things you mentioned."

No feature dump. No generic ROI slide. No company history.

Just: here's where we're different, and here's why it matters for you.

That's the whole pitch.

---

**Why this post works:**  
Dunford's *Sales Pitch* book is essentially a long-form version of this post. The format - observational story with dialogue - is her least frequent format but highest-performing. The contrast (listening first vs. feature dumping) lands because it's viscerally familiar to every buyer who's sat through a bad demo.

**Pattern:** "The best X I've seen" hook → dialogue → contrast with bad behavior → distillation to core insight

## Post 5 - "Why 'we do X for Y' positioning statements fail"

**Date:** ~Q3 2024  
**Format:** Text post  
**Engagement:** ~2,400 reactions, 280 comments  

---

"We help [ICP] achieve [outcome] by [mechanism]."

Every positioning template you'll find looks like this.

The problem: it describes what you do without saying why anyone should choose you.

I can build this sentence for your 5 closest competitors and it will sound exactly the same.

Positioning templates are not positioning. They're positioning-shaped holes where positioning should be.

Real positioning answers the question: "Why you instead of the alternatives?"

A template can't answer that. Only deep knowledge of your competitive landscape can.

Do the work.

---

**Why this post works:**  
Challenges one of the most widely-shared frameworks in B2B marketing (the "we help X achieve Y" template) with a specific, undeniable critique. Gets high engagement from people who have used this template and felt its weakness.

**Pattern:** Present the consensus framework → acknowledge its prevalence → specific failure mode → why it's structurally broken → what the real answer requires → punchy closer
