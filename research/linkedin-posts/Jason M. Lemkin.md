## Post 1

### No, Inbound Isn't Dead

**Date:** ~Q2 2025

**Format:** Text post

**Engagement:** ~3,100 reactions, 490 comments

**Source Context:** Referenced in LinkedIn profile activity feed, June 2025

### Post

> No, Inbound Isn't Dead.
>
> The GTM playbook isn't broken.
>
> Here's what's actually happening:
>
> The companies saying "inbound is dead" have one of three problems:
>
> They've cut marketing too deeply
>
> They've let brand quality slip
>
> They've stopped creating content that teaches something
>
> The inbound playbook is the same as it was 5 years ago: be so useful that people find you and trust you before they ever talk to you.
>
> The difference: it's harder now because everyone is running the same playbook. The quality bar went up.
>
> 10x better content. Or 10x more specific. Or both.

### Why this post works

Takes a "hot take" circulating in the SaaStr community and provides a grounded counter. Lemkin's voice is deliberately un-polished, conversational, and confident - which makes him feel like a peer sharing a real opinion rather than a speaker delivering a talk.

`Pattern: Direct title/refutation → name what's actually happening → 3-item diagnostic → restates the principle → identifies what changed → two-option prescription`

## Post 2

### You Have $100M ARR, 100%+ NRR. Why Are You Quitting?

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Storytelling + lesson

**Engagement:** ~4,800 reactions, 720 comments

**Source Context:** Referenced in LinkedIn activity, Jan 2025 (https://www.linkedin.com/in/jasonmlemkin/)

### Post

> Another founder I know - one running a company at $100M+ ARR - just announced on LinkedIn they were leaving. Stepping down.
>
> I've seen this more than once now.
>
> $100M ARR. 100% NRR. Great team. A decade of compounding.
>
> And they want out.
>
> Why?
>
> They're burned out. Not from the work - from the expectation.
>
> At $100M ARR, everyone assumes the company runs itself. It doesn't. The problems get bigger, not smaller. The stakes are higher, not lower. The loneliness is more acute, not less.
>
> The things that made them builders - the energy of early chaos, the speed, the small team - are gone. Now it's processes and org charts and board decks.
>
> If you're a founder under $20M ARR: this is what you're building toward. Know that some of you will love it. And some won't.
>
> There's no right answer. But there is more to build than just revenue.

### Why this post works

Lemkin's best content operates at the emotional layer that pure business tactics content never reaches. Posts like this get shared because founders identify with it privately but rarely say it publicly. The closing acknowledgment ("some won't") is rare - it doesn't sell the founder journey.

`Pattern: Observed case → surprise fact → why question → emotional root cause → what changed → honest reality check for readers earlier in journey → no-agenda closer`

## Post 3

### CROs at Slow-Growth Companies Aren't Really CROs

**Date:** ~Q2 2025

**Format:** Short hot take

**Engagement:** ~2,700 reactions, 430 comments

**Source Context:** LinkedIn profile, 2025 activity

### Post

> CROs at Slow-Growth Companies Often Aren't Really CROs. They're Chief Price Raising Officers.
>
> When a SaaS company slows down to 15% growth:
>
> New logo sales slow
>
> Expansion from product stagnates
>
> So leadership raises prices on existing customers
>
> And the CRO takes credit for "growth."
>
> This isn't scaling. It's extracting.
>
> A real CRO grows the market. Prices their customers into churning faster is not strategy.

### Why this post works

The wordplay in the title (CRO → "Chief Price Raising Officers") is memorable and slightly acidic. Lemkin punches at a behavior - not a person - which keeps it feel-good to share and hard to defend against.

`Pattern: Witty redefinition → mechanism that produces this behavior → why it's not real growth → 2-line contrast`

## Post 4

### What I've Learned After 13 Years of Building SaaStr

**Date:** ~Late 2024

**Format:** Long-form list

**Engagement:** ~7,200 reactions, 1,100 comments

**Source Context:** Reflected in LinkedIn profile bio and SaaStr content, 2025

### Post

> Here's what I've learned after 13 years of building SaaStr, investing in 50+ leading B2B companies, and watching thousands of founders:
>
> The best founders are obsessive. Not balanced. Not measured. Obsessive about one problem.
>
> The second hire matters more than the first. Your first hire is often luck. Your second is judgment.
>
> Most churn is a sales problem, not a product problem. You sold it to the wrong customer.
>
> NRR above 120% makes almost everything else easier. It compounds.
>
> The worst hires are people who are great at being hired. Run a work trial, not just interviews.
>
> Content compounds. The SaaStr blog from 2013 still drives traffic. Every post you write is infrastructure.
>
> At $5M ARR, your biggest problem is execution. At $25M, it's management. At $75M, it's processes. These are different problems requiring different skills.
>
> Investors remember how you behave in hard times more than good times. So do employees.
>
> The founder who talks to customers the most almost always wins.
>
> Build content from day one. Even if nobody reads it. You'll regret not starting sooner.

### Why this post works

"What I've learned after X years" is one of the highest-performing formats on LinkedIn. The specificity (13 years, 50+ companies) makes it authoritative. Each item is one rule that stands alone. Item 6 ("Every post you write is infrastructure") is the most shared single line.

`Pattern: Authority anchor → numbered rules → each is one sentence or a one-liner pair → mix tactical + philosophical → compounding-related closer`

## Post 5

### Want to learn how to get AI Agents to really work?

**Date:** ~Q1–Q2 2025

**Format:** Event/workshop post

**Engagement:** ~2,100 reactions, 380 comments

**Source Context:** From LinkedIn profile, 2025 (https://www.linkedin.com/in/jasonmlemkin/)

### Post

> Want to learn how to get AI Agents to really work? Really automate inbound with AI? Automate RevOps with AI?
>
> We're running a SaaStr Workshop Wednesday on this.
>
> Here's what I actually know after deploying 20+ AI agents at SaaStr:
>
> Most AI agent deployments fail in the first 30 days because:
>
> The handoff from AI to human is unclear
>
> Nobody owns the output quality
>
> The data the agent works from is dirty
>
> Success metrics were never defined
>
> The agents that work:
>
> → Have a human reviewer loop for the first 90 days
>
> → Operate on clean, well-labeled data
>
> → Are scoped to one job (not everything)
>
> → Have a clear definition of "success" before launch
>
> The failure mode is treating AI agents like employees. They're not employees. They're processes.

### Why this post works

Lemkin is one of the most credible voices on SaaS operations specifically, and this post grounds AI deployment in operational reality. "20+ AI agents at SaaStr" gives him specific authority. The "failure mode" section is the most quoted part.

`Pattern: Question hook (teaches while selling event) → credibility anchor → failure mode list → success pattern list → conceptual reframe`
