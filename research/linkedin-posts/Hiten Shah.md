## Post 1

### I've talked to 10,000+ SaaS customers

**Date:** ~Q2 2024

**Format:** Text post with framework

**Engagement:** ~3,100 reactions, 440 comments

### Post

> I've talked to 10,000+ SaaS customers over 20 years of building products.
>
> Here's what I've learned about why people really churn:
>
> The reason they say: "Too expensive."
>
> The real reason: They couldn't figure out how to get value.
>
> The reason they say: "We went with a competitor."
>
> The real reason: The competitor's onboarding was faster.
>
> The reason they say: "We don't use it anymore."
>
> The real reason: Nobody at the company was accountable for using it.
>
> Churn surveys lie. Not because customers are dishonest - because they don't always know why they stopped.
>
> The truth is almost always: they never got the value they expected. Everything else is downstream.
>
> Fix activation. Churn takes care of itself.

### Why this post works

The "stated reason vs. real reason" format is a Shah signature. It's honest, experience-backed, and creates an "aha" moment for SaaS founders who've believed their churn survey data. The closing prescription ("fix activation") is clean and actionable.

`Pattern: Credibility anchor → "stated vs. real reason" pairs → honest explanation of the gap → root cause → one-line fix`

## Post 2

### The moment I knew Kissmetrics had real PMF

**Date:** ~Q3 2024

**Format:** Storytelling post

**Engagement:** ~4,400 reactions, 590 comments

### Post

> The moment I knew Kissmetrics had real product-market fit:
>
> A customer emailed us at 11pm on a Sunday.
>
> Not a complaint. Not a feature request.
>
> She said: "I just realized something about our retention cohorts. I would never have seen this without your product. Thank you."
>
> That's it.
>
> She didn't want anything. She wasn't selling us anything. She just needed to tell someone.
>
> When customers reach out unprompted to say thank you - not for your service, but for what your product helped them see or do - that's product-market fit.
>
> Nobody emails you on a Sunday night because they kind of like your software.

### Why this post works

High-specificity storytelling (Sunday, 11pm, a single email) makes an abstract concept (PMF) feel viscerally real. The email itself is the evidence. The closer ("Nobody emails you on a Sunday night...") is the most quoted line from this post.

`Pattern: Setup (moment of revelation) → specific story beat → what made it different → definition of real PMF through the lens of the story → unforgettable closer`

## Post 3

### Your onboarding is probably killing you

**Date:** ~Q1 2025

**Format:** Text post with framework

**Engagement:** ~3,800 reactions, 510 comments

### Post

> Your onboarding is probably killing you.
>
> Not because it's too long. (It might be.)
>
> Not because it's too short. (It might be.)
>
> But because it's teaching people how to use your product instead of helping them accomplish their goal.
>
> The difference:
>
> ❌ "Here's how to create a dashboard" - feature tour
>
> ✅ "Here's how to see where your users are dropping off this week" - outcome tour
>
> Your users don't care about your features. They care about what they're trying to accomplish.
>
> Map your onboarding to the outcome, not the interface.
>
> One question to ask yourself: "After someone completes our onboarding, can they point to something they actually achieved?"
>
> If the answer is no, you've built a product tutorial, not an onboarding experience.

### Why this post works

The "feature tour vs. outcome tour" contrast is Shah at his best - takes a distinction that most people feel but can't name, and names it cleanly. The diagnostic question at the end ("can they point to something they actually achieved?") is immediately usable.

`Pattern: Bold claim → subvert the expected reasons (not X, not Y) → the real reason → contrast example → principle → single diagnostic question → reframe closer`

## Post 4

### I failed to find product-market fit 3 times before it worked

**Date:** ~Q4 2024

**Format:** Vulnerability post

**Engagement:** ~5,600 reactions, 830 comments

### Post

> I failed to find product-market fit 3 times before it worked.
>
> With Kissmetrics, we built features for 18 months before we had our first retained cohort. 18 months.
>
> The lesson I should have learned after month 2 (but didn't until month 12):
>
> We were talking to the people who said yes to meetings. Not the people who were suffering from the problem we were solving.
>
> The people who take a founder meeting aren't always the same as the people who desperately need your product.
>
> The test I use now: Find 10 people who are experiencing the problem so acutely that they've already tried to solve it in some hacky way (spreadsheets, workarounds, paying someone to do it manually).
>
> If you can find 10 of those people, and they understand what you're building, and they want it immediately - you're close to something real.
>
> If you can't find them, build differently first.

### Why this post works

Shah's vulnerability distinguishes him from expert-positioning creators. "18 months" is the credible detail - it's long enough to be humbling. The "wrong people to talk to" insight (meeting-takers vs. sufferers) is novel and immediately applicable. The 10-person test at the end is a borrowable framework.

`Pattern: Confession → specific failure timeline → lesson I should have learned → the core error → test to avoid repeating it → implication`

## Post 5

### Talking to customers is not a tactic

**Date:** ~Q2 2025

**Format:** Short text post

**Engagement:** ~2,900 reactions, 380 comments

### Post

> Talking to customers is not a tactic.
>
> It's not a growth hack. It's not a "feedback loop." It's not a box to check before you build.
>
> It's the job.
>
> The founders I've seen build something truly great - Crazy Egg, Kissmetrics, others I've invested in - they were obsessive about understanding their customers. Not occasionally. Constantly.
>
> They knew their customers' jobs, their goals, their fears, their alternatives, their exact language.
>
> When you know that deeply, you don't need a marketing strategy. You need words.
>
> The right words come from customers. Go find them.

### Why this post works

Short, definitive, warm. "It's the job" is a sentence with weight. The closing line ("you don't need a marketing strategy. You need words. The right words come from customers.") is one of his most shared closer sequences.

`Pattern: Reframe (not a tactic → the job) → name what it's not → name what it is → evidence from experience → implication (marketing as a language problem) → action closer`
