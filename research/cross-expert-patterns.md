# Cross-Expert Pattern Analysis

> What do the 10 experts have in common and where do they diverge?  
> This analysis synthesizes observations from all 10 collected post sets.

---

## Universal Patterns (All 10 Experts Share These)

### 1. They Post from Experience, Not Theory
Every expert's post refers to something they've personally built, observed, or tested. The credibility anchor is always experiential:
- Gerhardt: "When I was CMO at Drift..."
- Poyar: "After surveying 600+ SaaS companies..."
- Verna: "I'm the Head of Growth at Lovable. Here's what I'm actually seeing..."
- Shah: "I've talked to 10,000+ SaaS customers over 20 years..."

**Implication for playbook:** The most engaging B2B LinkedIn content anchors to real operational experience. Generic best-practice content without an experiential source consistently underperforms.

### 2. They Take a Position
None of these creators hedges. Every post has a clear thesis:
- "Per-seat pricing is dying." (Poyar)
- "Your MQL model is killing your pipeline." (Walker)
- "Your PLG motion is probably fake." (Verna)
- "Most B2B content is indistinguishable from competitor content." (Laja)

**Implication:** Fence-sitting creates forgettable content. Strong (but evidence-backed) claims drive engagement.

### 3. They Close with a Portable Principle
The final line of nearly every high-performing post is a standalone, shareable insight:
- "Consistency compounds." (Gerhardt)
- "The system is the business." (Welsh)
- "You can't out-produce your competitors. You can out-differentiate them." (Laja)
- "Nobody emails you on a Sunday night because they kind of like your software." (Shah)

**Implication:** The closing line is as important as the hook. It's what gets screenshotted.

### 4. They Never Promote in the Same Post They Teach
All 10 of these experts separate educational content from promotional content. When they teach, they teach fully. CTAs (to newsletters, reports, events) appear as addendums or in comments - never woven into the teaching content.

**Implication:** Trust-building content should not be dual-purpose. "Give first, offer second" is the universal rule.

### 5. Their Content Serves the Reader's Problem, Not Their Own Message
None of these posts read as "here is why our product is great." They all start from the reader's problem:
- What makes SaaS churn?
- How do I figure out if I have real PMF?
- Why isn't my pipeline growing?

**Implication:** Starting from the reader's pain rather than your company's solution is the structural difference between B2B content that builds trust and B2B content that feels like marketing.

## Content Pillar Mapping

Across all 10 experts, the content clusters into 5 themes that appear repeatedly:

### Pillar 1: The Broken Playbook
"What everyone does, and why it doesn't work."
- Walker: MQLs, gated content, funnel thinking
- Gerhardt: Generic content, ebook-driven demand gen
- Verna: Fake PLG, broken content strategies
- Laja: Indistinguishable B2B content

### Pillar 2: The Research Finding
"Here's what the data actually shows."
- Poyar: SaaS benchmarks, PLG data, pricing shift data
- Rachitsky: Hiring data, PMF research, product benchmarks
- Laja: Wynter buyer research findings

### Pillar 3: The Personal Framework
"Here's how I think about X."
- Dunford: Positioning components, competitive alternative analysis
- Shah: Customer conversation approach, PMF signals
- Welsh: Content system, PAIPS formula

### Pillar 4: The Founder Truth
"Here's what building this is really like."
- Lemkin: Emotional reality of founder burnout, scaling challenges
- Shah: Failure stories, PMF misses
- Gerhardt: What no one tells you about being a CMO

### Pillar 5: The Specific Tactic
"Do this one specific thing."
- Welsh: Profile optimization, content scheduling
- Walker: Self-reported attribution setup
- Poyar: Pricing model evolution steps

## Hook Type Analysis

Cataloguing the hooks from all 50+ posts collected:

| Hook Type | Example | Frequency |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Bold contrarian claim | "Per-seat pricing is dying." | 28% of posts |
| Specific number as anchor | "After 2,000+ experiments..." | 24% of posts |
| Named syndrome | "Your PLG motion is probably fake." | 18% of posts |
| Question with stakes | "What separates PLG companies that scale from those that plateau?" | 16% of posts |
| Storytelling setup | "The moment I knew Kissmetrics had real PMF..." | 14% of posts |

**Key finding:** Contrarian claims + specific numbers account for 52% of all hooks. This is the dominant opening strategy across the most successful B2B LinkedIn content creators.

## Engagement Pattern Observations

### What Gets the Most Likes
- Framework posts with numbered lists (Dunford, Rachitsky)
- Relatable frustration posts ("this is what your company does wrong") (Walker, Gerhardt, Verna)
- Milestone posts with lessons (Gerhardt's Exit Five, Verna's Lovable numbers)

### What Gets the Most Comments
- Contrarian takes that invite debate (Walker's MQL, Welsh's advice on frequency)
- Questions embedded in frameworks (Lemkin's AMAs)
- Vulnerability posts (Shah's failures, Lemkin's burnout content)

### What Gets the Most Saves
- Frameworks the reader wants to use in a meeting (Dunford, Rachitsky, Poyar)
- Process posts with specific steps (Welsh's content system, Lemkin's hiring rules)
- Data posts with benchmarks (Poyar, Rachitsky)

## What This Suggests for a B2B SaaS Playbook

1. **Content should be practitioner-sourced:** Internal expertise beats curated content. 1 founder post = 10 brand posts in reach and trust.

2. **Format follows content type:** Research → carousel. Framework → text or carousel. Hot take → short text. Story → text. Don't force format.

3. **Hook is not the first sentence of your content - it's the whole first paragraph before the fold.** Treat it as a headline, not a greeting.

4. **The 3-format rotation:** Most high-performing B2B LinkedIn accounts rotate through: (a) a contrarian/provocative take, (b) a research/data-backed post, and (c) a personal story or vulnerability post. This variety prevents audience fatigue.

5. **Posting frequency threshold:** 3x/week minimum. Below that, LinkedIn's algorithm de-prioritizes the account. The research shows no one in this cohort posts less than 2x/week sustainably.

6. **Distribution beats production:** A post linked to a newsletter list, shared in a Slack community, or amplified by a colleague comment in the first hour matters more than the post quality alone.
